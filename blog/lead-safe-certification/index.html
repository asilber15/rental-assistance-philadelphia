Philadelphia Lead-Safe Certification: A Landlord's Guide

Updated June 2026 · A Rental Assistance Philadelphia guide

If you rent out an older home in Philadelphia, lead-paint compliance is one of the requirements most likely to catch you off guard. The City requires many rental properties to show they are lead-safe (or lead-free) — certified by an independent, qualified inspector — as a condition of renting. Here's a plain-English overview of how it works and where to confirm the details for your property.

Read this first. Lead rules are detailed and have changed over time, and the exact obligation depends on your property's age and circumstances. This guide is a general orientation, not a substitute for the City's official lead-disclosure and certification requirements. Always confirm your specific obligation with the City of Philadelphia before relying on anything here.

What lead-safe certification is

Lead-based paint was common in homes built before 1978, and it remains a serious health hazard — especially for young children. To protect tenants, Philadelphia requires many rental properties to demonstrate that they are either lead-free (no lead-based paint present) or lead-safe (any lead-based paint is intact and not a hazard), verified by a qualified, independent inspector. The result is a certificate the landlord keeps and provides as required.

Who it generally applies to

The requirement is generally tied to the age of the building and to residential rentals. Older homes — the kind that make up much of Philadelphia's rental stock — are the ones most likely to fall under it. Because the precise scope (which properties, which tenants, what triggers a new test) is set by City ordinance and has expanded over time, the safest approach is to assume an older rental may be covered and verify the specific requirement for your address.

How a property gets certified

  1. Engage a qualified, independent inspector

    Certification must come from a properly certified lead inspector or risk assessor — not a self-assessment. They evaluate the property for lead-based paint and lead hazards.

  2. Address any hazards found

    If the inspection identifies lead hazards, they have to be remediated or controlled so the property can be certified lead-safe.

  3. File and keep the certification

    The certification is documented and provided to tenants and the City as required, and renewed on the schedule the City sets.

How it ties to your rental license

Lead compliance doesn't stand alone — it sits alongside the rest of your Philadelphia licensing obligations. To legally rent, you need a valid Rental License (built on your Business Tax Account and, for most landlords, a Commercial Activity License), a Certificate of Rental Suitability for each new tenant, and to be current on city taxes and clear of open code violations. Lead-safe certification is part of operating a compliant, legally rentable property where it applies.

The cost of non-compliance

Failing to meet Philadelphia's lead requirements can expose a landlord to fines and enforcement, and — like other compliance gaps — can undermine your standing if you ever need to enforce an eviction. Beyond the legal exposure, the underlying risk is real: lead hazards can seriously harm tenants, especially children. Compliance protects them and you.

Common questions

Does every Philadelphia rental need a lead certificate?

Not every one — the requirement is generally tied to building age and residential rentals, and the exact scope is set by City ordinance. Older homes are most likely to be covered. Confirm your specific obligation with the City.

Can I certify the property myself?

No. Lead-safe or lead-free certification must come from a properly qualified, independent inspector or risk assessor.

How does this fit with my rental license?

It's part of the same compliance picture. You still need your Business Tax Account, Commercial Activity License (where required), Rental License, and a Certificate of Rental Suitability for each new tenant — lead compliance sits alongside those.
