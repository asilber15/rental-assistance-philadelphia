Why you need a Philadelphia rental license

Why you need a rental license in Philadelphia.

Without a valid Rental License you can't legally collect rent, you can't enforce an eviction, and you're racking up fines every month. The good news: it's tedious paperwork, but simple — and we handle all of it.

What happens if you don't have one

  • You can't legally collect rent. Renting without a valid license puts you out of compliance with the Philadelphia Property Maintenance Code from day one.
  • You can't enforce an eviction. Philadelphia courts can dismiss an eviction if you don't hold a valid Rental License and can't show you gave the tenant a Certificate of Rental Suitability.
  • You face fines and penalties. Operating unlicensed exposes you to L&I violations and per-day penalties that add up fast.
  • It quietly blocks everything downstream. A missing or lapsed license can stall a sale, a refinance, or a rent-assistance filing.

What you need to get licensed

  • A Business Tax Account (BIRT) with the Department of Revenue — the first link in the chain.
  • A Commercial Activity License, unless you qualify for the owner-occupied exception (below).
  • A valid Rental License from L&I — renewed every year before it lapses.
  • A Certificate of Rental Suitability, issued within 60 days before a new tenant moves in.
  • To be current on city taxes and clear of open code violations.
The Certificate of Rental Suitability, in plain terms: a document the City requires you to obtain within 60 days before a new tenant moves in, and to give to every new tenant when their lease starts — along with the City's partners-for-good-housing handbook. Skip it and you can lose the right to evict that tenant for nonpayment. We pull it as part of the license package.

This is the question most landlords search for — how to get a rental license in Philadelphia, what the requirements are, and why it matters. The honest answer: the requirements aren't complicated, but the order, renewals, and certificate trip people up. That's exactly the part we do for you.

Why you need a Commercial Activity License

Do you need a Commercial Activity License?

For most Philadelphia landlords, yes — the City treats renting as a business, so this license usually has to be in place before your Rental License can issue. It's free but mandatory, and it sits in a specific spot in the order. We sort out whether you need it and handle it if you do.

The order matters — do it in this sequence

  • 1. Business Tax Account / BIRT first. Open a Business Tax Account with the Department of Revenue before anything else. Nothing downstream issues without it.
  • 2. Commercial Activity License next. With the tax account in place, you obtain the Commercial Activity License from L&I.
  • 3. Then the Rental License. Only once the account and license are in place will L&I issue your Rental License.

Get the order wrong and the application stalls. We set up each link in sequence so it never bounces back.

What you need to know

  • It's free — but mandatory. No fee for the Commercial Activity License, yet you can't skip it (unless you qualify for the exception below).
  • You must be current on city taxes. The City won't issue it if you have outstanding filings or an open balance.
  • It's the gate to the Rental License. No Commercial Activity License (where required) means no Rental License means no legal right to collect rent.
The exception — 4 or fewer units, owner-occupied: if your building has 4 or fewer units and you (the owner) live in one of them, you do not need a Commercial Activity License. You still need the Business Tax Account and the Rental License — just not this middle step. Our intake asks for unit count and owner-occupancy so we route you the right way automatically.
Must be current on city taxes: a tax balance or unfiled return is the single most common reason a Commercial Activity License — and the Rental License behind it — gets blocked. If that's you, start with City Tax Compliance and we'll clear the hold first, then license.

Questions, answered

Back rent, licensing, and how it all works.

The eviction diversion program, Targeted Financial Assistance, the $3,500 cap, payment timing, the rental-license chain, and whether your case qualifies.

My tenant is behind on rent. Will the City really pay it?

Yes. Through the City's Eviction Diversion Program you can elect the Targeted Financial Assistance (TFA) track, under which the City pays your tenant's past-due rent (up to a $3,500 recovery cap) plus two months of future rent — by direct deposit to you. It's not a loan, and there's no application fee.

What is the Eviction Diversion Program (EDP)?

It's permanent Philadelphia law (Ordinance #220655, codified at Phila. Code § 9-811). Before filing most evictions, a landlord must send a notice of diversion rights and participate in good faith for at least 30 days. Within diversion, you can choose the TFA track instead of mediation — the track that gets you paid.

Is the $3,500 a limit on whether my tenant qualifies?

No — this is the most common misunderstanding. The $3,500 is a cap on the back rent the program will recover, not an eligibility ceiling. A tenant who owes more than $3,500 still qualifies. You settle the back-due balance at the $3,500 cap and still receive the two future months on top.

How is the money paid, and how long does it take?

Funds are paid by direct deposit into your bank account. The City says payment is possible within 30 days, though timing can vary case to case — across our real cases the median has run about 1.5 months.

Does this cost me anything?

The program costs the landlord nothing: it's not a loan, there's no repayment, and there's no application fee. The funds are an assistance payment, not credit against future rent.

What is the 60-day moratorium?

After TFA pays the two future months, a 60-day window follows during which you cannot file for eviction. The month you submit the rent ledger sets the window — submit in June, get paid for July & August, and September & October fall under the moratorium. See the timeline.

If it's free and available, why do I need a service?

The money isn't the hard part — the application is. It runs through the City's portal, needs specific documents in a specific order, and quietly stalls if anything is missing. The City's site gives you an application number but no single consolidated view of your cases. We run the whole filing and consolidate every application into one view — so it never stalls on a missing document.

Will my tenant have to move out?

Often not. With the back rent cleared and two months paid ahead, many tenancies reset and the tenant stays. When a case isn't resolved, diversion tends to channel even hard cases toward a negotiated, mutual outcome rather than a forced lockout.

Why do I need a rental license in Philadelphia?

To legally rent property in Philadelphia you must hold a valid Rental License from Licenses & Inspections (L&I). Without it you can't legally collect rent, you can't enforce an eviction, and you're exposed to fines and per-day penalties for operating unlicensed. The license must be renewed every year, and you must give each new tenant a Certificate of Rental Suitability obtained within 60 days before move-in. That's the licensing & compliance side of what we do.

What are the rental-license requirements, and how do I get one?

Getting a Philadelphia rental license is a chain done in order: first a Business Tax Account (BIRT) with the Department of Revenue, then a Commercial Activity License (unless you have 4 or fewer units and live in one), then the Rental License from L&I, plus a Certificate of Rental Suitability for each new tenant. You must also be current on city taxes and clear of open code violations. We set up each step in sequence and obtain the certificate as part of the package.

Do I need a Commercial Activity License?

Most Philadelphia landlords do. Renting property is treated as operating a business, and a Commercial Activity License is generally required before a Rental License can issue. It's free but mandatory, you must be current on city taxes to obtain it, and it comes after your Business Tax Account and before your Rental License. The exception: if your building has 4 or fewer units and you (the owner) live in one of them, you don't need a Commercial Activity License — though you still need the Business Tax Account and the Rental License.
